AI for Robotics

    History of Robotics in AI

    Ancient Foundations (Pre-20th Century)

    ~300BC

    Early Automata

    • Archytas of Tarentum: Mechanical bird powered by steam (~350 BCE)
    • Hero of Alexandria: Automated temple doors, mechanical theater (1st century CE)
    • Al-Jazari: Programmable humanoid automata (1206)
    • Leonardo da Vinci: Mechanical knight design (1495)
    18-19th

    Industrial Revolution

    • Jacques de Vaucanson: Mechanical duck (1739), automated loom
    • Joseph Jacquard: Programmable loom using punch cards (1804)
    • Charles Babbage: Analytical Engine (1837) - precursor to computers
    • Nikola Tesla: Radio-controlled boat (1898)

    The Birth of Modern Robotics (1920-1969)

    1921

    Term "Robot" Coined

    Czech playwright Karel Čapek introduces the term "robot" in his play R.U.R. (Rossum's Universal Robots), derived from the Czech word "robota" meaning forced labor.

    1942

    Asimov's Three Laws

    Isaac Asimov publishes "Runaround," introducing the Three Laws of Robotics that would influence robotics ethics for decades:

    1. A robot may not injure a human or allow harm through inaction
    2. A robot must obey human orders (unless conflict with 1st Law)
    3. A robot must protect its own existence (unless conflict with 1st/2nd Laws)
    1956

    Birth of AI

    The Dartmouth Summer Research Project on Artificial Intelligence marks the official birth of AI as a field, with participants including John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, and Nathaniel Rochester.

    1961

    First Industrial Robot

    Unimate, the first digitally operated and programmable robot, is installed at a General Motors plant in New Jersey to handle hot metal parts. This marks the beginning of industrial robotics.

    AI Robotics Revolution (1970-1999)

    1972: WABOT-1

    The first full-scale anthropomorphic robot built at Waseda University in Japan. It could walk, grip objects, and communicate in Japanese.

    1979: Stanford Cart

    One of the first autonomous vehicles, navigating a chair-filled room without human intervention over 5 hours.

    1986: LEGO Mindstorms

    The first programmable robotics kit for education, making robotics accessible to students and hobbyists.

    1997: Deep Blue vs. Kasparov

    IBM's chess-playing computer defeats world champion Garry Kasparov, demonstrating AI's strategic capabilities.

    1997: Sojourner on Mars

    The first successful Mars rover, demonstrating autonomous navigation on another planet.

    1999: Sony AIBO

    The first consumer entertainment robot with learning capabilities, paving the way for social robotics.

    Modern Era (2000-Present)

    Robotics Breakthroughs

    • 2000: ASIMO by Honda - advanced humanoid walking
    • 2004: DARPA Grand Challenge sparks autonomous vehicle research
    • 2011: Watson wins Jeopardy! showcasing NLP
    • 2012: First robotic exoskeleton approved by FDA
    • 2015: OpenAI founded to ensure safe AI development

    AI Advancements

    • 2012: AlexNet revolutionizes deep learning
    • 2014: GANs (Generative Adversarial Networks) introduced
    • 2015: TensorFlow released by Google
    • 2017: Transformer architecture enables modern NLP
    • 2020: GPT-3 demonstrates few-shot learning

    Current Trends

    Collaborative Robots (Cobots)

    Robots designed to work safely alongside humans in shared workspaces.

    Embodied AI

    AI systems with physical bodies that learn through environmental interaction.

    Soft Robotics

    Flexible, adaptable robots inspired by biological systems.

    Multi-Agent Systems

    Coordinated teams of robots working together on complex tasks.

    Neuromorphic Engineering

    Hardware designed to mimic biological neural systems.

    Future Directions

    Versatile machines capable of performing multiple tasks in unstructured environments.

    →

    Artificial Consciousness

    Robots with self-awareness and subjective experiences (controversial but active research).

    →

    Molecular Robotics

    Nanoscale robots for medical applications and material science.

    →
