Agile and Scrum

    What is Scrum in Agile?

    Definition

    Scrum is an Agile framework for developing, delivering, and sustaining complex products. It is a lightweight, iterative approach that helps teams deliver value incrementally in a collaborative manner. Scrum is founded on empirical process control theory and emphasizes transparency, inspection, and adaptation.

    Scrum Origins

    • First introduced in 1986 by Hirotaka Takeuchi and Ikujiro Nonaka
    • Formalized for software development by Jeff Sutherland and Ken Schwaber in 1990s
    • First presented at OOPSLA conference in 1995
    • Published in the Scrum Guide (current version from 2020)

    Scrum vs Agile

    • Agile is a philosophy (values and principles)
    • Scrum is a specific framework that implements Agile
    • Scrum is the most popular Agile methodology (58% of Agile teams use Scrum)
    • Other Agile methods include Kanban, XP, Lean, etc.

    Scrum Framework Components

    1. Scrum Roles

    Scrum Master

    Servant-leader who ensures Scrum is understood and enacted

    Product Owner

    Maximizes product value by managing the Product Backlog

    Development Team

    Cross-functional group that delivers increments

    2. Scrum Events

    Sprint

    Time-boxed iteration (typically 1-4 weeks)

    Sprint Planning

    Plan work for the upcoming Sprint

    Daily Scrum

    15-minute daily coordination meeting

    Sprint Review

    Inspect the increment and adapt backlog

    Sprint Retrospective

    Improve processes and practices

    3. Scrum Artifacts

    Product Backlog

    Ordered list of everything needed in the product

    Sprint Backlog

    Set of Product Backlog items selected for the Sprint

    Increment

    Sum of all completed Product Backlog items

    Benefits of Scrum

    For Organizations

    • Faster time to market
    • Higher product quality
    • Better ROI
    • Increased customer satisfaction

    For Teams

    • More autonomy
    • Better collaboration
    • Continuous improvement
    • Clearer focus

    For Stakeholders

    • More transparency
    • Frequent delivery
    • Early feedback opportunities
    • Better risk management

    When to Use Scrum

    Ideal Situations

    • Complex product development
    • Requirements likely to change
    • Need for frequent delivery
    • Cross-functional teams
    • Innovative products

    Challenging Situations

    • Highly regulated environments
    • Fixed-price contracts
    • Teams resistant to change
    • Very small projects
    • Non-iterative work
