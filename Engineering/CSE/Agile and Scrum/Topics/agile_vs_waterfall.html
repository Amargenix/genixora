Agile and Scrum

    Agile vs Waterfall

    Fundamental Differences

    Development Approach

    Waterfall: Linear, sequential phases where each phase must be completed before the next begins.

    Agile: Iterative and incremental approach with work divided into small, manageable units (sprints).

    Requirements Handling

    Waterfall: Requirements gathered upfront and frozen early in the project.

    Agile: Requirements expected to evolve and change throughout the project.

    Customer Involvement

    Waterfall: Customer involved primarily at beginning and end.

    Agile: Continuous customer collaboration throughout the project.

    Delivery Timing

    Waterfall: Working product delivered at the end of the project.

    Agile: Working increments delivered at regular intervals (1-4 weeks).

    Methodology Comparison Table

    Aspect Waterfall Agile
    Project Structure Sequential phases (Requirements, Design, Implementation, Verification, Maintenance) Iterative cycles (Sprints) with all phases in each iteration
    Change Management Changes difficult and expensive after requirements phase Changes expected and welcomed even late in development
    Documentation Extensive documentation required upfront Working software valued over comprehensive documentation
    Risk Management Risks addressed during planning phase Continuous risk management throughout project
    Team Structure Hierarchical with specialized roles Self-organizing, cross-functional teams
    Progress Measurement Against plan and documents Working software and customer satisfaction
    Best For Projects with clear, fixed requirements (construction, manufacturing) Projects with uncertain or evolving requirements (software, creative)

    Advantages and Disadvantages

    Waterfall Advantages

    • Clear structure with well-defined milestones
    • Easy to manage due to rigidity
    • Good for projects with fixed requirements
    • Documentation is thorough and maintained
    • Easy to measure progress against plan
    • Works well for small projects with clear objectives

    Waterfall Disadvantages

    • Inflexible to changes after requirements phase
    • Working product not available until late
    • High risk and uncertainty
    • Poor model for complex, object-oriented projects
    • Difficult to estimate time and cost accurately
    • Integration done at the end (big bang approach)

    Agile Advantages

    • Flexible to changing requirements
    • Working software delivered frequently
    • Continuous customer involvement
    • Early and predictable delivery
    • Continuous improvement via retrospectives
    • Focus on business value and user needs

    Agile Disadvantages

    • Difficult to predict final product
    • Requires significant customer involvement
    • Not suitable for all organizations/cultures
    • Documentation may be neglected
    • Requires experienced team members
    • Final cost may exceed traditional approach

    When to Use Each Methodology

    Use Waterfall When:

    • Requirements are very well documented, clear, and fixed
    • Product definition is stable
    • Technology is understood and not dynamic
    • There are no ambiguous requirements
    • The project is short and simple
    • Regulatory requirements demand extensive documentation

    Use Agile When:

    • Requirements are not well understood or expected to change
    • You need to deliver value quickly
    • Customers are available and willing to collaborate
    • The project is complex with many unknowns
    • You need frequent feedback and course correction
    • The team is experienced with Agile practices

    Hybrid Approaches

    Water-Scrum-Fall

    Combines Waterfall planning and requirements with Agile development sprints, followed by Waterfall-like testing and deployment phases.

    Best for:

    Organizations transitioning to Agile but with regulatory or compliance requirements.

    Agile-Stage-Gate

    Combines Agile's iterative development with Stage-Gate's phase review process for go/no-go decisions.

    Best for:

    Product development where investment decisions need formal review points.

    Lean Startup + Agile

    Combines Agile development with Lean Startup's build-measure-learn feedback loop for innovation.

    Best for:

    Startups and new product development with high uncertainty.
