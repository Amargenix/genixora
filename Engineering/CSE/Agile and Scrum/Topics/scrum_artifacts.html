Agile and Scrum

    Scrum Artifacts in Agile

    What Are Scrum Artifacts?

    Scrum artifacts represent work or value to provide transparency and opportunities for inspection and adaptation. The three formal Scrum artifacts are:

    1. Product Backlog
    2. Sprint Backlog
    3. Increment

    These artifacts are designed to maximize transparency of key information so that everybody has the same understanding.

    1 Product Backlog

    Definition: An ordered list of everything that might be needed in the product and the single source of requirements for any changes.

    Key Characteristics

    • Dynamic and constantly evolving
    • Prioritized by the Product Owner
    • Contains all features, functions, requirements, enhancements, and fixes
    • Items at the top are more refined than those at the bottom

    Product Backlog Item Types

    User Stories Bugs Technical Work Spikes Enablers

    2 Sprint Backlog

    Definition: The set of Product Backlog items selected for the Sprint plus a plan for delivering them.

    Key Characteristics

    • Created during Sprint Planning
    • Owned by the Development Team
    • Highly visible, real-time picture of the work
    • Contains tasks needed to convert PBIs into a Done Increment

    Sprint Backlog Components

    Selected PBIs Tasks Workflow Progress

    3 Increment

    Definition: The sum of all completed Product Backlog items during a Sprint plus all previous Sprints.

    Key Characteristics

    • Must be in usable condition
    • Meets the Scrum Team's Definition of Done
    • Potentially releasable (at Product Owner's discretion)
    • Demonstrated at Sprint Review

    Increment Attributes

    Integrated Tested Documented Done

    Additional Artifacts & Concepts

    Definition of Done (DoD)

    A shared understanding of what it means for work to be complete. The DoD ensures transparency and quality.

    • Code reviewed
    • Unit tests passed
    • Integration tested
    • Documentation updated

    Burndown Charts

    Visual representations of work left to do versus time.

    • Sprint Burndown (daily progress)
    • Release Burndown (Sprint-to-Sprint)
    • Shows if team is on track

    Velocity

    A measure of how much work a team can complete in a Sprint.

    • Calculated in story points
    • Used for forecasting
    • Team-specific metric

    Artifact Transparency

    Scrum relies on complete transparency of these artifacts. Decisions to optimize value and control risk are made based on the perceived state of the artifacts.

    Product Backlog Transparency

    • Clear prioritization
    • Items at top are ready for selection
    • Visible to all stakeholders

    Sprint Backlog Transparency

    • Real-time status visible
    • Daily updates in Scrum
    • Team ownership clear

    Increment Transparency

    • Meets Definition of Done
    • Demonstrable functionality
    • Quality is transparent

    Best Practices for Scrum Artifacts

    Product Backlog

    • Refine regularly (backlog grooming)
    • Keep items at top detailed and estimated
    • Use relative estimation techniques
    • Collaborate with stakeholders

    Sprint Backlog

    • Update daily during standups
    • Make visible to entire team
    • Break down items into tasks
    • Limit work in progress

    Increment

    • Deliver something usable every Sprint
    • Don't accumulate technical debt
    • Invest in automation
    • Continuously improve Definition of Done

    Scrum Artifacts in the Agile Context

    These artifacts embody Scrum's empirical approach to product development. They enable inspection and adaptation through:

    • Transparency: Clear visibility into progress and status
    • Inspection: Frequent examination of artifacts and progress
    • Adaptation: Adjustments based on inspection results

    "Scrum's artifacts represent work or value to provide transparency and opportunities for inspection and adaptation." - Scrum Guide
