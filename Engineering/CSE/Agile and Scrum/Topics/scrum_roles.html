Agile and Scrum

    Scrum Roles in Agile and Scrum

    Introduction to Scrum Roles

    Scrum defines three specific roles that form the core team structure. These roles work together to deliver value through iterative development cycles called Sprints.

    Product Owner

    The voice of the customer and business

    Scrum Master

    The facilitator and process coach

    Development Team

    The builders and creators

    The Product Owner (PO)

    Key Responsibilities

    • Defines and communicates product vision
    • Creates and maintains the Product Backlog
    • Prioritizes backlog items based on value
    • Ensures backlog transparency and clarity
    • Makes final decisions on release content and timing
    • Represents stakeholder interests
    • Accepts or rejects work results

    Characteristics

    • Single person (not a committee)
    • Strong decision-making authority
    • Deep understanding of customer needs
    • Available to the team
    • Understands both business and technical aspects
    • Empowered to make product decisions

    Common Anti-Patterns

    Proxy PO

    Someone without real decision-making authority acting as PO

    Absent PO

    PO not available to answer team questions

    Backlog Neglect

    Not maintaining or prioritizing the backlog

    The Scrum Master

    Primary Responsibilities

    • Ensures Scrum is understood and enacted
    • Removes impediments to team progress
    • Facilitates Scrum events
    • Coaches the team in self-organization
    • Shields the team from external interruptions
    • Promotes continuous improvement
    • Works with the organization to adopt Scrum

    Servant Leadership

    The Scrum Master serves the:

    • Development Team: Coaching, removing blockers
    • Product Owner: Backlog techniques, stakeholder management
    • Organization: Leading Scrum adoption

    Common Misconceptions

    Not a Project Manager

    Doesn't manage people or assign tasks

    Not a Secretary

    Doesn't just take notes or schedule meetings

    Not a Technical Lead

    Doesn't make technical decisions for the team

    The Development Team

    Key Characteristics

    • Self-organizing (decides how to do the work)
    • Cross-functional (all skills needed to deliver)
    • No titles or sub-teams within the team
    • Typically 3-9 members
    • Accountable for delivering increments
    • Collaborative and focused

    Responsibilities

    • Delivering potentially shippable increments
    • Estimating work items
    • Managing their own work
    • Ensuring quality
    • Adapting based on feedback
    • Improving their process

    Team Composition

    Skills

    Developers, testers, designers, analysts - whatever skills needed to deliver

    T-Shaped

    Deep expertise in one area, but able to contribute in others

    Collaboration

    Works together daily, shares knowledge, pairs when needed

    PO ↔ Dev Team

    • PO provides clear requirements
    • Team provides estimates and feedback
    • Collaborative refinement sessions
    • Regular demos and feedback loops

    Scrum Master ↔ All

    • Coaches PO on backlog management
    • Facilitates team self-organization
    • Helps organization understand Scrum
    • Removes impediments for all

    Other Stakeholders (Non-Scrum Roles)

    Customers/Users

    Provide feedback, use the product, define value

    Managers

    Provide resources, remove organizational impediments

    Subject Matter Experts

    Consulted as needed for specialized knowledge

    Important Notes

    • These stakeholders don't have direct authority over the Scrum Team
    • They provide input through the Product Owner
    • The Scrum Master helps manage these relationships
