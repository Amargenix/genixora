    Servant Leadership

    What is Servant Leadership?

    Servant leadership is a philosophy where the leader's primary goal is to serve their team. This approach:

    • Focuses on the growth and well-being of team members
    • Prioritizes team needs over the leader's own agenda
    • Builds trust and empowers individuals
    • Creates a culture of collaboration and shared purpose

    Origin

    The concept was coined by Robert K. Greenleaf in 1970, inspired by Hermann Hesse's novel Journey to the East where the group's servant was actually their leader.

    Servant Leadership in Agile Context

    Why Agile Needs Servant Leaders

    • Agile values individuals and interactions over processes
    • Self-organizing teams require empowerment, not control
    • Complex work demands collaborative problem-solving
    • Continuous improvement needs psychological safety

    Manifesto Alignment

    • "Individuals and interactions over processes and tools"
    • "Customer collaboration over contract negotiation"
    • "Responding to change over following a plan"

    "The servant-leader is servant first... It begins with the natural feeling that one wants to serve, to serve first." - Robert K. Greenleaf

    Servant Leadership in Scrum Roles

    1 Scrum Master as Servant Leader

    Primary Focus

    • Serving the Development Team
    • Serving the Product Owner
    • Serving the organization

    Key Behaviors

    • Removing impediments
    • Facilitating Scrum events
    • Coaching rather than directing
    • Shielding the team from distractions

    Mindset Shift

    From "I'm in charge" to "How can I help?"

    From giving answers to asking powerful questions

    2 Product Owner as Servant Leader

    Primary Focus

    • Serving customers and stakeholders
    • Serving the Development Team
    • Maximizing product value

    Key Behaviors

    • Clear communication of vision
    • Being available to the team
    • Making timely decisions
    • Balancing stakeholder needs

    3 Development Team as Servant Leaders

    Primary Focus

    • Serving the product and customers
    • Serving each other
    • Delivering quality increments

    Key Behaviors

    • Collaborative decision-making
    • Knowledge sharing
    • Helping teammates
    • Collective ownership

    10 Characteristics of Servant Leaders

    1. Listening

    Genuinely paying attention to team members' concerns and ideas without judgment.

    2. Empathy

    Understanding and sharing the feelings of others, walking in their shoes.

    3. Healing

    Helping to resolve conflicts and supporting emotional recovery.

    4. Awareness

    Being mindful of team dynamics and one's own impact on others.

    5. Persuasion

    Building consensus through reasoning rather than authority.

    6. Conceptualization

    Seeing the big picture and helping others understand it.

    7. Foresight

    Anticipating consequences and guiding the team accordingly.

    8. Stewardship

    Taking responsibility for the team's work and environment.

    9. Commitment to Growth

    Dedicated to the personal and professional growth of each team member.

    10. Building Community

    Fostering a sense of belonging and shared purpose.

    Servant Leadership in Practice

    Daily Activities

    • Asking "How can I help?" instead of giving orders
    • Removing organizational impediments
    • Facilitating rather than leading meetings
    • Celebrating team successes
    • Creating safe spaces for experimentation

    Powerful Questions

    • "What do you need to be successful?"
    • "How can we improve this process?"
    • "What obstacles are you facing?"
    • "What would great look like here?"
    • "How can I support you better?"

    Servant Leadership vs Traditional Leadership

    Aspect Traditional Leader Servant Leader
    Focus Organizational goals Team needs and growth
    Decision Making Top-down Collaborative
    Power Held by leader Shared with team
    Success Measure Results achieved Team growth and results
    Primary Role Director Facilitator

    Practical Exercise: The Serving Day

    Commit one day per week where your only focus is serving your team. During this day:

    • Ask each team member "How can I help you today?"
    • Focus on removing impediments
    • Document what you learn about team needs
