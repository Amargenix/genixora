Agile and Scrum

    Agile Estimation Techniques

    Why Agile Estimation Differs

    Traditional estimation focuses on time (hours/days), while Agile estimation focuses on relative sizing and complexity using abstract units.

    Traditional Estimation

    • Time-based (hours/days)
    • Individual contributor estimates
    • Fixed scope assumptions
    • Detailed upfront planning

    Agile Estimation

    • Relative sizing (story points)
    • Team-based consensus
    • Variable scope flexibility
    • Progressive refinement

    Key Estimation Techniques

    Story Points

    Abstract units representing effort, complexity, and risk. Typically uses Fibonacci sequence (1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 20, 40, 100).

    Example: Implementing login screen = 3 points, Payment integration = 8 points

    Planning Poker

    Consensus-based technique where team members privately select cards representing their estimate, then discuss differences.

    Process: 1. Present story ‚Üí 2. Discuss ‚Üí 3. Vote ‚Üí 4. Repeat until consensus

    T-Shirt Sizing

    Relative sizing using XS, S, M, L, XL for quick high-level estimates, often used for roadmap planning.

    Scale: XS (1pt), S (2-3pts), M (5pts), L (8pts), XL (13+pts)

    Bucket System

    Fast estimation method where items are sorted into predefined buckets (e.g., 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 20 points).

    Best for: Large backlogs needing quick estimation

    Affinity Mapping

    Grouping user stories by relative size through silent sorting, then assigning point values to groups.

    Advantage: Visual comparison of all items

    Dot Voting

    Team members allocate dots to represent effort/complexity, with more dots indicating larger size.

    Variation: Use different colored dots for risk/uncertainty

    The Estimation Process

    1. Baseline Story

    Establish a reference story the team agrees is "3 points" to compare all other stories against.

    2. Relative Sizing

    Compare new stories to baseline: "Is this twice as hard? Then it's probably 5 points."

    Estimation Factors to Consider

    Effort

    How much work is involved?

    Complexity

    How difficult is the work?

    Uncertainty

    How many unknowns exist?

    Estimation Do's and Don'ts

    Do

    • Estimate as a team
    • Compare to known stories
    • Consider all aspects (dev, test, etc.)
    • Re-estimate when new info emerges

    Don't

    • Convert points to hours
    • Let managers dictate estimates
    • Anchor on previous estimates
    • Ignore uncertainty

    Velocity and Capacity Planning

    Velocity

    The average number of story points a team completes per sprint. Calculated after 3-5 sprints.

    Example: If team completes 24, 26, 22 points ‚Üí Avg velocity = 24

    Capacity

    The total available working hours for a sprint, considering holidays, PTO, and other commitments.

    Calculation: (Team members √ó Work hours/day √ó Sprint days) - Unavailable time

    Advanced Estimation Concepts

    Spikes

    Time-boxed research tasks to reduce uncertainty before estimating. Typically 2-8 hours.

    Reference Stories

    Maintain a catalog of previously completed stories with their point values for comparison.

    Re-Estimation

    When stories change significantly or new information emerges, re-estimate to maintain accuracy.

    Estimation Anti-Patterns

    • Padding estimates: Building in buffer "just in case"
    • Anchoring: Being influenced by first number suggested
    • Expert estimation: Relying on one person's opinion
    • Reverse engineering: Starting from desired date
    • Ignoring variance: Not accounting for natural fluctuation
    • Point inflation: Gradually increasing estimates over time
