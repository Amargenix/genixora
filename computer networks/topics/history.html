Computer Networks

    History of Computer Networks

    Early Concepts (Pre-1960s)

    Telegraph Networks

    The first electrical communication networks were telegraph systems (1830s-1840s). Samuel Morse developed Morse code in 1838, enabling long-distance communication.

    Telephone Networks

    Alexander Graham Bell patented the telephone in 1876. By the 1890s, telephone networks with manual switchboards were established in major cities.

    Early Computing

    In the 1940s-1950s, computers were large, isolated machines. The concept of time-sharing (multiple users accessing a single computer) emerged in the late 1950s.

    SAGE System

    The Semi-Automatic Ground Environment (SAGE) air defense system (1958) was one of the first large-scale computer networks, connecting radar sites to command centers.

    The Birth of Packet Switching (1960s)

    Paul Baran's Work

    At RAND Corporation (1960-1964), Paul Baran developed the concept of distributed, packet-switched networks for military communications that could survive nuclear attacks.

    ARPANET

    The Advanced Research Projects Agency Network (ARPANET), funded by the U.S. Department of Defense, was the first operational packet-switching network (1969). The first nodes were at UCLA, SRI, UCSB, and University of Utah.

    NPL Network

    Donald Davies at the UK's National Physical Laboratory independently developed packet-switching concepts and built a local area network (1965-1967).

    Interface Message Processors

    ARPANET used dedicated minicomputers called IMPs (Interface Message Processors) as network routers, with the first IMP installed at UCLA in 1969.

    Network Protocols and Expansion (1970s)

    TCP/IP Development

    Vinton Cerf and Robert Kahn developed the Transmission Control Protocol (TCP) in 1974, which later split into TCP and IP (Internet Protocol).

    Ethernet

    Robert Metcalfe at Xerox PARC developed Ethernet in 1973, which became the standard for local area networks (LANs).

    Email

    Ray Tomlinson implemented the first email system on ARPANET in 1971, using the @ symbol to separate user and host names.

    International Connections

    The first international connections to ARPANET were established in 1973 with nodes in Norway and the UK.

    The Internet Emerges (1980s)

    TCP/IP Adoption

    In 1983, ARPANET switched from NCP to TCP/IP as its standard protocol, marking the birth of the Internet.

    DNS Introduction

    The Domain Name System (DNS) was introduced in 1984, replacing numerical addresses with human-readable names.

    NSFNET

    The National Science Foundation Network (NSFNET) was created in 1985, connecting supercomputing centers and becoming the backbone of the Internet.

    Commercialization Begins

    The first commercial Internet service providers (ISPs) emerged in the late 1980s as restrictions on commercial use were lifted.

    World Wide Web and Explosive Growth (1990s)

    Tim Berners-Lee's Invention

    At CERN in 1989-1990, Tim Berners-Lee developed the World Wide Web, creating HTTP, HTML, and the first web browser/editor.

    Mosaic Browser

    The Mosaic web browser (1993) was the first to display images inline with text, making the web more accessible to non-technical users.

    Commercial Boom

    The mid-1990s saw explosive growth of commercial websites, online services (AOL, CompuServe), and e-commerce (Amazon 1994, eBay 1995).

    Broadband Emerges

    Cable modems and DSL began replacing dial-up connections in the late 1990s, enabling faster Internet access.

    Modern Era (2000s-Present)

    Web 2.0

    The 2000s saw the rise of user-generated content (Wikipedia 2001, YouTube 2005), social media (Facebook 2004, Twitter 2006), and cloud computing.

    Mobile Revolution

    The introduction of smartphones (iPhone 2007) and mobile apps transformed how people access networks, with mobile traffic surpassing desktop by 2016.

    High-Speed Networks

    Fiber-optic networks, 4G/LTE (2010s), and now 5G (2020s) provide increasingly faster wireless connectivity.

    IoT and Future Trends

    The Internet of Things (IoT) connects billions of devices. Emerging technologies include edge computing, AI-driven networks, and quantum networking research.
